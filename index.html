The Washing Machine
Wasn't the Problem

A failed delivery led me to a deeper flaw in how ecommerce understands customers. Platforms are remarkably intelligent before checkout and surprisingly blind after it. This case study proposes a 0→1 feature — Mission-Aware Commerce — that closes that gap for high-involvement categories, starting with a single optional question.

The incident

A question that seemed obvious and wasn't

Wednesday. 4:47 PM. I was at my desk when my phone buzzed. Delivery attempted. Customer unavailable.

I live in Thane and commute into Mumbai for work. I leave home by 7:30 AM and don't get back until 7 PM. The delivery arrived at 4:47 PM, found no one home, and left. It was rescheduled. A few days later the same thing happened. I finally received the washing machine six days after the original delivery date, on a Saturday when I happened to be home all morning.

My first reaction was frustration. My second was a question I could not let go of. Why didn't the platform simply ask when I would be available?

It felt obvious. Add a time slot at checkout. Morning, afternoon, evening. Done. I was almost ready to write that up and move on. Then I started looking into how last-mile logistics actually works, and the obvious fix stopped holding up.

First hypothesis, and why I killed it

The obvious solution was wrong
at the scale that matters

A delivery driver in a dense metro handles between 40 and 80 stops per shift. Their route is optimised at 6 AM based on geographic clustering. The algorithm sequences stops to minimise travel time across all deliveries, not to accommodate individual time preferences.

If every customer selects a delivery window, routes stop being routes. They become a set of conflicting constraints. Driver productivity falls. Cost per delivery rises. Reliability goes down. The feature that solves one frustrated customer's problem creates a worse system for millions of others.

The obvious customer solution was not the right business solution. That distinction is where the real product thinking started.

So I went back to the problem. If I had misidentified the solution, I had probably misidentified the problem too.

Reframing

Not all deliveries are
the same kind of thing

Most deliveries in my apartment complex do not fail. The security guard collects packages. Neighbours accept parcels. People are absent from home during the day across the entire building, yet the majority of orders arrive on the first attempt.

So why did mine fail? The answer is not that I was unavailable. The answer is that my product required me to be present in a way that a book or a phone case does not.

Low involvement

  • Clothing, books, accessories
  • Requires transportation only
  • Security guard can accept it
  • Journey ends at handoff
  • Done when package changes hands

High involvement

  • Appliances, furniture, large electronics
  • Requires customer to be present
  • Needs installation or setup
  • Needs building access coordination
  • Done when product is actually working

The fulfillment infrastructure treats both categories identically. Same dispatch logic, same routing, same definition of success: package handed over. For low-involvement products that works. For high-involvement products it is incomplete in a way that guarantees the failure I experienced.

But even this was not the deepest version of the problem. I was still thinking about product types. The real insight came when I stopped asking what I bought and started asking why.

The core insight

Transaction-aware.
Mission-blind.

Before checkout, Amazon is remarkably intelligent about customers. It tracks browsing, comparisons, saved items, purchase history. Recommendation engines surface relevant products before customers consciously want them. The entire pre-purchase experience is built around understanding intent.

The moment checkout is complete, that intelligence stops. What replaces it is a fulfillment system that knows inventory, warehouse, route, driver, and delivery date. The customer has been reduced from a person with a goal to an address on a manifest.

Amazon knew I purchased a washing machine. It did not know whether my existing machine had broken, whether I had just moved, whether this was a planned upgrade, or whether timing mattered to me at all. Three customers can buy the exact same product on the same day and their situations could not be more different.

All three enter the same fulfillment flow. All three receive the same experience. For Customer C this is fine. For Customer A it is a failure waiting to happen, and the platform has no way to tell the difference.

This is what mission-blind means. The platform understands the transaction. It does not understand what the customer came to accomplish.

Root cause

Tracing the failure
back to its source

Why did delivery fail?

The customer was not home.

Why was the customer not home?

They were at work. The delivery arrived at 4:47 PM. They return home at 7 PM.

Why did they not plan around the delivery?

They knew the date. They did not know the time. There was no way to find out in advance.

Why did the platform not ask when the customer was available?

Because that field does not exist. Checkout collects an address. It never collects availability or purchase intent.

Why does the platform not know the reason for purchase?

Because post-purchase intelligence was never a design goal. The system was built to move products efficiently, not to understand why the purchase was made.

The failure originates not at the doorstep but at checkout, in the absence of one question that would have changed how the order was handled from the moment it was placed.

The opportunity

Mission-Aware Commerce

The next evolution of ecommerce post-purchase is not faster delivery. It is smarter delivery, meaning delivery that understands what the customer is actually trying to accomplish.

The premise is not that platforms should collect more data. It is that they should collect one specific, high-signal piece of data they currently do not collect at all: the reason behind the purchase. Not demographics, not preferences, not another account field to fill in. A single optional question, shown after purchase confirmation for high-involvement categories, that gives the platform enough context to route the post-purchase experience meaningfully.

The intervention

A single optional question on the post-purchase confirmation screen, shown only for appliances, furniture, and large electronics:

One tap. Optional. Placed after checkout so it adds zero friction to the purchase decision. The response is not stored for quarterly analysis. It immediately triggers a differentiated fulfillment experience.

The reason it lives after checkout and not during it is simple. Checkout is the highest-friction moment in the journey. A ₹25,000 appliance purchase is not the moment to add questions. Post-purchase confirmation is different. The transaction is done. The customer is in a moment of anticipation, and the cost of not answering is zero.

What each signal unlocks

Same product.
Three different responses.

Broken product signal

The system registers urgency. Expedited delivery options surface, even with a small premium. Installation gets proactively scheduled at the time of order confirmation rather than waiting for the customer to find a separate booking flow. If a delay occurs, the order is flagged for proactive outreach before the customer has to call. The platform treats it as a restoration, not a routine purchase.

New home signal

The system enables coordination. Delivery can be set three to five weeks out, aligned with a move-in date. Multiple appliances ordered for the same home can be bundled into one delivery window. The experience is built around a life event rather than a single transaction.

Gift signal

Status notifications go to the buyer, not to the delivery address. Surprise is protected by default. The delivery window can be adjusted without the buyer needing to intercept the standard tracking flow.

Upgrade signal

Standard fulfillment. No change needed. The existing system handles this well and there is no reason to add complexity where it is not necessary.

The signal does not need to be perfect to be useful. Even if 40 percent of customers respond, the platform now has directional information about urgency and expectations for a meaningful share of high-involvement orders. That is substantially better than the zero information it currently operates on.

Decision

What I chose to build
and what I ruled out

Guaranteed time slot at checkout Rejected

Breaks driver route efficiency at scale. A failed guarantee at the moment of highest customer anticipation is worse than no guarantee at all. Right destination, wrong timing.

Mission signal question at checkout Rejected

Abandonment risk on high-value orders. The question belongs after purchase where friction cost is zero, not inside the conversion funnel.

Post-purchase mission signal with differentiated routing Chosen

Optional question after order confirmation. No conversion risk. Immediately triggers a differentiated fulfillment experience. Starts with the broken product signal only, where urgency is clearest and the operational response is most straightforward. Expandable as logistics integration matures.

Safe drop and neighbour delivery Rejected

Treats the symptom. Creates theft and liability risk. Makes COD inspection worse. Moves the failure mode rather than removing it.

Phase 1

6 weeks

In progress

Signal collection, no routing change yet

Launch the post-purchase question for washing machines and refrigerators across the top 10 metro pin codes. No fulfillment change yet. Validate that customers engage and that signals correlate with actual reschedule rates. Everything that comes after depends on this holding up.

Success: 35% or more response rate. Signal distribution matches expected urgency patterns.

Phase 2

4 months

Planned

Routing differentiation for broken product signal

For orders tagged as broken product replacement, surface expedited delivery options and proactively trigger installation scheduling at order confirmation. Measure Time To Outcome for tagged orders against a control group. Only builds once Phase 1 shows the signal is trustworthy.

Success: 20% or more TTO improvement for tagged orders versus control.

Phase 3

9 months

Planned

New home and gift signals

Build future scheduling and bundled delivery for the new home signal. Build notification separation for the gift signal. Expand category coverage to ACs, furniture, and large electronics. The scope here is deliberately broad because Phase 2 will sharpen what we actually need to build.

Success: Positive TTO improvement across all active signal types.

Phase 4

12 months+

Planned

Account-level mission preferences

Allow customers to set persistent context — moving in March, frequently replacing broken appliances — that auto-populates mission signals for future eligible purchases. This is the long-term destination. It only makes sense to build it after customers have already experienced the value of the signal in earlier phases.

Success: 15% or more of high-involvement orders using persistent preference.

Metrics Evolving

Time To Outcome,
not time to delivery

Most ecommerce metrics measure movement: orders dispatched, packages delivered, hours in transit. These measure the platform's output. The right metric for mission-aware commerce measures the customer's outcome.

Time To Outcome is the time between purchase confirmation and the customer's first successful use of the product for its intended purpose. A washing machine's TTO ends at the first completed laundry cycle, not when the package changes hands. This metric is harder to instrument than delivery time. That difficulty is the point. It forces product, logistics, and customer experience teams to align around what the customer actually came to accomplish.

Tradeoffs

What I would not build,
and why that matters

✕

Mandatory mission question

The moment it becomes required it produces resentment and inaccurate answers. The feature's value depends entirely on customers choosing to engage with it.

✕

Mission signals for low-involvement categories

The operational cost of differentiating fulfillment for a ₹299 book is not justified. This belongs only in high-involvement categories and must stay there.

✕

Urgency-based delivery as a paid premium tier

Charging more because someone's machine broke is extractive. Urgency-based prioritisation should be a service improvement, not a revenue line.

✕

Expanding the question to collect more signals

One honest answer about purchase intent is worth more than ten fields filled in at account setup and never updated. Signal quality over signal quantity.

Two risks worth naming directly

Signal gaming is real. If customers learn that selecting "broken product" results in faster delivery, some will select it opportunistically. This is managed by not showing the outcome of each mission selection during the question step, and by monitoring signal distribution over time for anomalies.

Logistics partner readiness is a genuine constraint. Differentiated routing requires integration with couriers who may not be ready to honour mission-based prioritisation uniformly. Phase 2 should launch only in pin codes where Amazon-operated logistics has sufficient density to support it without relying on third parties.

Reflection

What this taught me
about problem framing

I started with a missed delivery. I thought I was solving a scheduling problem. Then a logistics problem. Then a coordination problem. Each time I found a solution that held up technically but felt incomplete, I went back to the problem rather than forward to the feature.

That discipline, of resisting the pressure to ship something buildable when the right thing has not been found yet, is the most transferable lesson from this project.

Sometimes the biggest opportunity is not improving an existing process. It is questioning whether the system is optimising for the right outcome in the first place.

The delivery failure was a symptom. The real problem was that ecommerce platforms stop trying to understand the customer the moment checkout is complete. A system that knows what you bought but not why you bought it will always produce mismatches between what it delivers and what the customer actually needed.

I am still sitting with some of this. The metrics framework in particular will look different once Phase 1 generates real data. I will update this document as the thinking sharpens.

The one-paragraph pitch

Ecommerce platforms invest enormous resources in understanding customers before checkout. The moment a purchase is complete, that understanding stops. Three customers can buy the same washing machine on the same day: one because theirs broke, one because they are moving, one as an upgrade. All three receive an identical post-purchase experience. I want to add a single optional question after order confirmation for high-involvement categories that asks why the purchase was made. That signal routes the post-purchase experience toward what the customer actually needs: urgency for replacements, coordination for moves, flexibility for gifts. The north star is not faster delivery. It is Time To Outcome, the gap between purchase and first use. Close that gap for high-value categories and you build a post-purchase experience that no competitor can easily replicate.
